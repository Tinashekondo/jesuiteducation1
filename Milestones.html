Milestones In Jesuit Education

St. Ignatius of Loyola (1491-1556)
• Conversion experience in 1521
• Spiritual Exercises, 1524
• Ordained priest, 1537
• Society of Jesus Founded, 1540

1542: The Jesuit open colleges (residences) in the Universities of Coimbra, Padua, Louvain and cologne only for Jesuits.
1543: Goa: Few Jesuits teaching in a seminary: reading, writing, grammar, catechism (Primary education)
1546: Jesuits begin to teach students who were not Jesuits at the request of the Duke of Gandia.
1547: Officials at Messina petition for a college
1548: Ignatius sends 10 Jesuits headed by Fr. Nadal to open the Collegio di San Nicolo in Messina
1565: 30 colleges in Italy
1569: The Ratio Studiorum Borgiana
1581: 150 Schools
1586: The first official Ratio (a draft)
1591: A revised version of the Ratio
1599: The Official Ratio Studiorum is promulgated. 245 schools
1773: The Pope Clement XIV suppressed the Society. 546 schools in Europe and 123 missions (especially in Hispanic America & India. 4 schools survived thanks to Catherine II, the Great, in Russia
1814: The Pope Pius VII restored the Society
1833: 45 schools
1960: 4,059 schools (according to Allan Farrell SJ
2012: 2182 schools (Jesuit Schools, Fe y Alegria, Universities

The Society of Jesus was not founded as a teaching order, but the mission eventually brought the Jesuits to the frontiers of education.

1965 – 1966: GC 31- The Importance of the Education Apostolate.
1974 – 1975: GC 32 - Every apostolate must be permeated with faith that does justice and with the option for the poor.
1978:- Fr. Arrupe’s Letter on “ The Secondary Schools Today and the Tomorrow
1979 - Colloquium / SIP movement begun
1980 - The establishment of the International Commission for the Apostolate of Jesuit Education (ICAJE).The work begins on the Characteristics Document.
1986 - The Characteristics of Jesuit Education
1993 - The Ignatian Pedagogy, A Practical Approach Document issued.

The Mission of Jesuit Education: The mission of a Jesuit school is to educate and form students to learn to their fullest potential and to become self-reliant, critical thinkers, socially sensitive, lifelong learners, appreciative of diverse culture, alive to the needs of our world, with the knowledge which gives them power to act, to lead productive lives and to become contributing members of the global community. (Unofficial)
